Summary
Automated Essay Scoring (AES) systems have emerged as powerful tools to
streamline the evaluation of student essays by leveraging machine learning and
natural language processing techniques. Despite their growing adoption in ed-
ucational settings, several critical challenges remain in ensuring the reliability ,
fairness, and practical usefulness of AES systems. This thesis addresses these
challenges by critically evaluating the limitations of current AES methodologies,
exploring fairness considerations, and exploring feedback mechanisms that en-
hance robustness and actionability in AES systems, particularly through coun-
terfactual explanations.
This thesis is structured around four key areas of investigation:
Chapter 4 critically examines the limitations of relying solely on human-
computer agreement as the primary evaluation metric for Automated Essay
Scoring (AES) systems. While models with high agreement scores are often
considered successful, this chapter presents empirical evidence that such mod-
els may still exhibit serious reliability issues when exposed to challenging input
scenarios—such as off-topic essays, gibberish responses, and paraphrased an-
swers. Specifically , the chapter shows that even AES models meeting all stan-
dard quantitative performance thresholds (e.g., Quadratic Weighted Kappa) fail
in two out of three validity tests, raising concerns about their readiness for op-
erational deployment.
To address these shortcomings, the chapter explores two strategies for im-
proving AES robustness. First, augmenting the training data of prompt-specific
models with off-topic (from other prompts) and gibberish responses signifi-
cantly enhances the model’s ability to detect and penalize anomalous inputs,

vi
without degrading its overall scoring performance. Second, the use of a simple
rule-based filter improves gibberish detection, offering a lightweight yet effec-
tive safeguard.
Overall, the findings underscore the limitations of agreement-based eval-
uation and advocate for broader testing frameworks that include inputs that
fall outside the typical training distribution, such as off-topic or nonsensical
responses. For AES systems to be safely and fairly deployed, they must demon-
strate robustness not only on standard test sets but also in handling inputs that
challenge the integrity of automated scoring.
Chapter 5 investigates the use of Quadratic Weighted Kappa (QWK) as a
standard performance metric for evaluating AES systems. While QWK is widely
adopted for its ability to account for chance agreement on ordinal scales, this
chapter identifies several important limitations in its application. These include
its sensitivity to rating scales, its behavior under the so-called kappa para-
dox—where high percent agreement may still yield low QWK scores—and its
vulnerability to class distribution imbalances and the positioning of agreement
values in the confusion matrix. The analysis is grounded in experiments using
the ASAP dataset and, while context-specific, the findings raise broader con-
cerns about the stability and interpretability of QWK across different scoring
scenarios.
In response, the chapter explores complementary strategies, such as incorpo-
rating Linear Weighted Kappa (LWK) and combining multiple evaluation metrics
to provide a more comprehensive view of model quality . Rather than advocat-
ing for the replacement of QWK, the chapter encourages a more cautious and
contextualized use of this metric in AES research and practice. It underscores
the importance of aligning evaluation choices with the structure of the scoring
task, thereby promoting more robust and interpretable assessments.
Chapter 6 introduces a methodology for evaluating individual fairness in
AES systems. Unlike group-level fairness, which focuses on ensuring fairness
across demographic groups, individual fairness ensures that similar essays re-
ceive similar scores. The need for individual fairness has been highlighted in
prior work. Bejar et al. [BFFR14] demonstrated that automated scoring systems
can be manipulated through construct-irrelevant strategies, such as substituting
words with rarer synonyms. This allows essays of similar quality to receive dif-
ferent scores depending on superficial lexical choices, raising concerns about
consistency at the individual level. Kumar et al. [KPS+23] found that AES mod-
els can be oversensitive to small changes and overstable to substantial content
edits, revealing inconsistencies in how similar essays are scored.
To address this, the chapter compares various text representations—such as

vii
Bag-of-Words (BOW), TF-IDF, Sentence-BERT, and LASER—and scoring mod-
els including Ridge Regression, Random Forest, and Gradient Boosting, in their
ability to maintain fairness. The study evaluates consistency in scoring across
similar essays using cosine-based similarity measures and a set of fairness-
related criteria. Among the combinations tested, Sentence-BERT paired with
Gradient Boosting achieved strong agreement with human scores and showed
favorable behavior across the fairness evaluation metrics, including a smooth
correlation between essay similarity and score difference. This chapter proposes
a flexible evaluation framework for individual fairness, demonstrating how text
similarity and score consistency can be jointly analyzed to better understand
AES behavior in educational contexts.
Chapter 7 introduces an exploratory approach to improving feedback in AES
through the use of counterfactual explanations supported by Large Language
Models (LLMs). The system provides personalized, actionable feedback by gen-
erating both positive and negative counterfactual examples, suggesting minimal
but meaningful changes that can lead to improved essay scores. Positive coun-
terfactuals highlight how specific improvements (e.g., better grammar, spelling,
or word choice) may raise scores, whereas negative counterfactuals illustrate
how certain errors could lower scores. This dual approach is intended to en-
hance model robustness and help students better understand the consequences
of both revising and neglecting aspects of their writing.
Additionally , the chapter proposes a practical label-generation technique for
regression-based essay scoring, addressing the unique challenges of counter-
factual sample generation. This technique supports more consistent scoring of
counterfactuals, enabling the system to refine its predictions more effectively .
A survey conducted with prospective teachers yielded generally positive re-
sponses, with participants finding the feedback meaningful while also suggest-
ing a more user-friendly interface and additional interactive features. Overall,
the chapter positions LLM-supported AES feedback as a promising direction that
warrants further classroom-based evaluation to confirm its educational impact.
This thesis makes four key contributions across its core research questions
and experimental studies:
• Empirical evidence demonstrating that high human-computer agreement
alone is insufficient to ensure reliable AES performance, particularly in di-
verse input scenarios such as off-topic essays and gibberish. Strategies, in-
cluding retraining models with off-topic and gibberish data, are proposed
to improve model robustness.
• An empirical investigation into the behavior of Quadratic Weighted Kappa

viii
(QWK) in AES evaluation, highlighting its limitations with respect to rat-
ing scale sensitivity , prevalence, and the kappa paradox. Based on analy-
ses using the ASAP dataset, the thesis suggests incorporatingLinear Weighted
Kappa (LWK) and multiple evaluation metrics to support more robust and
context-sensitive AES model assessments.
• A methodology for assessing individual fairness in AES, which includes
evaluating text representation techniques and regression models. The
methodology analyzes whether similar essays receive consistent scores
by considering multiple fairness-related criteria, including similarity rank-
ings, feature-based discrimination, and score consistency across essay pairs.
• The development of an innovative web-based platform that integrates
counterfactual explanations using both positive and negative examples,
supported by LLMs. This platform offers personalized, actionable feed-
back to students, enabling incremental essay improvements. The system
includes a novel label generation technique for regression-based essay
scoring, improving the model’s robustness and the quality of feedback.
User surveys validated the platform’s effectiveness while highlighting ar-
eas for further enhancement.
The findings of this thesis highlight the need for more comprehensive eval-
uation methods in AES systems, considering not only agreement with human
scores but also fairness and the potential to support student learning outcomes.
Future research directions include further exploration of multi-modal approaches,
improving feedback mechanisms through more advanced LLMs, and extending
the proposed methodologies to other educational applications. These efforts
aim to create more reliable, fair, and educationally valuable AES systems that
can enhance the learning experience for students.

1.1 Automated Essay Scoring
The role of written essays in education is well-established, serving as a key
tool for assessing student understanding, critical thinking, and communication
skills [HHJH +23, QK07, ASASHA20]. Whether in high-stakes testing environ-
ments or classroom-based assessments, essays provide a platform for students
to articulate ideas, defend arguments, and demonstrate mastery over complex
topics. However, the process of grading essays is notoriously time-consuming
and labor-intensive [ZB17, BBE+21, AAJH19]. Human evaluators are prone to
inconsistencies due to fatigue, bias, and subjective interpretation of grading
rubrics. As educational systems expand—with growing student populations
and increasingly diverse learning contexts—manual essay grading becomes un-
sustainable. This has led to the rise of automated solutions like Automated
Essay Scoring (AES) systems, which aim to alleviate the burden on educa-
tors by offering efficient, consistent, and scalable essay assessment [OOO23].
AES refers to the computational process of assigning a score—or set of rubric-
based ratings—to a written essay without human intervention, using natural-
language-processing and machine-learning models trained on previously scored
samples [Pag66,RS22,KN19].
AES leverages machine-learning and NLP techniques to deliver rapid, con-
sistent evaluations of student writing, reducing grading time and labour costs
[MJS+23,GH17]. Earlier AES systems based on feature engineering and statisti-
cal or machine-learning models were already shown to achieve high agreement

2 Introduction
with human raters under standard evaluation conditions, particularly in large-
scale assessment contexts [AB06, YBM11]. Subsequent advances in neural and
pre-trained language models further improved AES performance and robust-
ness, extending beyond hand-crafted features toward end-to-end representa-
tions of writing quality [LN24]. More recently , the emergence of generative AI
and large language models (LLMs) has renewed interest in AES, with studies
reporting comparable levels of agreement and reliability when LLMs are used
alongside human raters, while also revealing new sources of variability and un-
certainty [PBE24,MAEE24]. These operational benefits make AES an attractive
solution for educational settings where human grading is impractical or incon-
sistent.
Despite these operational benefits, the broader adoption of AES systems is
hindered by unresolved challenges related to their reliability , fairness, and ped-
agogical validity . High human–machine agreement, typically measured with
metrics such as Quadratic Weighted Kappa, may hide brittle behaviour on ad-
versarial or off-topic inputs [Zir23]. Moreover, construct-irrelevant cues and
scorer fatigue complicate any simple comparison to human grading standards
[WXB12, BB98]. These gaps motivate the present thesis, which investigates
robustness, individual-level fairness, and actionable feedback mechanisms for
neural AES models. A fuller discussion of the operational benefits and known
pitfalls of AES appears in Section 2.2.2.
This research focuses on Automated Essay Scoring (AES)—the automatic
assignment of holistic or analytic (rubric-based) scores to full-length student
essays using machine learning and natural language processing. The thesis in-
vestigates three major concerns affecting the reliability and educational value of
AES systems: (i) the limitations of performance metrics like Quadratic Weighted
Kappa, (ii) the detection of individual fairness violations using textual similarity
and scoring disparity diagnostics, and (iii) the use of counterfactual explana-
tions to generate feedback that can guide student revisions in a more actionable
way . In this context, counterfactual suggestions may include limited grammar
or wording changes, provided they are generated with the goal of increasing the
predicted score. Broader Automated Writing Evaluation (AWE) functions—such
as stand-alone grammar correction or stylistic enhancement tools—fall outside
the scope of this thesis, except where they have a direct and measurable impact
on the essay score. These focus areas are further developed in the problem state-
ment and scope of the thesis (Section 1.3), and revisited across the empirical
chapters that follow.

1.2 Impact of AES on Different Stakeholders 3
1.2 Impact of AES on Different Stakeholders
Automated Essay Scoring (AES) systems influence the teaching and learning
landscape in important and multifaceted ways. Beyond their technical capa-
bilities, these systems carry broader implications for education, affecting how
students receive timely feedback, how educators allocate time and effort in as-
sessment, and how institutions and policymakers define evolving standards and
expectations for writing instruction and evaluation. This section explores the
potential benefits and challenges of AES implementation across different levels
of the educational system.
1.2.1 Impact on Students
AES systems provide students with faster feedback on their writing, enabling
them to reflect and improve their work in real time. This is especially bene-
ficial in formative contexts, such as classroom-based writing exercises, where
timely feedback supports iterative learning and revision [WG08,ZL20]. In large
classes, where instructors may lack the capacity to offer individualized guid-
ance, AES can help bridge the feedback gap [Dık06,GH17].
However, the effectiveness of AES as a formative tool is limited by the scope
and quality of the feedback it provides. Many systems emphasize surface-
level features—such as grammar, word count, and sentence complexity—while
overlooking deeper aspects of writing like creativity , voice, or argument qual-
ity [Per14]. As a result, students may struggle to develop higher-order writing
skills when relying heavily on automated feedback [WW06].
In summative contexts, where AES is used to assign grades or determine
high-stakes outcomes, fairness concerns become more pronounced. Studies
have raised questions about potential demographic biases in AES systems, par-
ticularly those affecting students from underrepresented or multilingual back-
grounds [WW06]. If students perceive the scoring process as opaque or unfair,
it can erode trust in the educational system [MJS +23]. These risks underscore
the importance of developing AES systems that are not only accurate but also
transparent and equitable.
1.2.2 Impact on Educators
For educators, AES systems offer a practical solution to the time-intensive pro-
cess of grading essays. By automating repetitive tasks, educators can focus on

dents struggling with writing. In some contexts, educators may be involved in

1.2.3 Impact on Educational Institutions
Automated Essay Scoring (AES) systems have been widely used in a vast num-

view, many exams and school districts already mix computer and human scor-

same way . Bringing AES into a school, however, still requires paying licensing

1.2.4 Impact on Policymakers
For policymakers, AES systems represent an opportunity to enhance the scala-
bility and objectivity of educational assessments. By integrating AES into stan-
dardized testing frameworks, policymakers can address challenges related to
human grader inconsistencies and expand access to high-quality assessment
tools. However, the adoption of AES also raises critical policy questions about
accountability , transparency , and the equitable distribution of these technolo-
gies. In particular, emerging European and national regulations on artificial
intelligence and automated decision-making introduce constraints on the use of
AES in high-stakes educational contexts, requiring clear human oversight. Pol-
icymakers must establish guidelines to ensure that AES systems are rigorously
evaluated and aligned with broader educational objectives.
1.2.5 Societal Implications
Beyond immediate stakeholders, the broader societal impact of AES encom-
passes both potential benefits and challenges. On the positive side, AES can
increase access to automated feedback tools, offering more students timely
evaluations and supporting educational equity by providing consistent assess-
ments across diverse populations. However, the reliance on machine-generated
feedback may , in some systems, inadvertently emphasize surface-level features
such as grammar, vocabulary , and mechanics, while overlooking more sub-
jective and human-centric aspects like creativity , critical reflection, and voice
[LLYH25, HHN19]. This could lead to a redefinition of writing values, narrow-
ing the focus of writing instruction and impacting the development of critical
thinking skills.
1.3 Scope and Problem Statement
Although AES can dramatically reduce grading workloads, persistent gaps in
reliability , fairness, and feedback constrain its educational value [WXB12]. This
section details those gaps and frames the thesis problem statement.

6 Introduction
This thesis focuses on English-language essays written by middle- and secondary-
school students (grades 7–12), specifically within the context of standardized
educational assessment. It targets multi-paragraph argumentative, expository ,
or narrative essays ranging from 150 to 650 words, as represented in the widely
used ASAP dataset (see Section 3.2.1). Essays from other learner groups, shorter
constructed responses, and professional or domain-specific writing are outside
the scope of this study . While issues such as plagiarism and text reuse are rele-
vant to the validity of student-produced writing, this thesis assumes that submit-
ted essays constitute original student work, in line with standard AES evaluation
settings, and does not explicitly address plagiarism detection or prevention. The
research investigates how AES systems perform in this educational context, with
particular attention to issues of reliability , fairness, and feedback mechanism.
Reliability. Human–computer agreement is commonly used as a benchmark
for evaluating AES performance, yet metrics such as Quadratic Weighted Kappa
(QWK) [Coh68] can report high scores while masking unreliable behavior on
varied inputs [AB06]. Students may “game” the system—writing longer texts
or adding connectors—to raise scores without improving quality [CC08], and
even nonsensical essays may receive high marks [PBC +01]. These issues raise
concerns about overreliance on agreement-based metrics and highlight the need
for more robust evaluation practices.
Fairness. Most prior work in AES fairness focuses on group fairness—the idea
that demographic groups (e.g., by race, gender, or language background) should
receive comparable treatment from the scoring system. In contrast, individ-
ual fairness—the expectation that similar essays receive similar scores—has re-
ceived less attention [DSPP22]. This gap raises concerns about score consis-
tency at the individual level, especially when textual similarity does not trans-
late to comparable outcomes.
Feedback. Students benefit most from assessments that not only assign a score
but also support meaningful revision. However, many existing AES systems
focus primarily on score prediction and provide limited actionable guidance for
improvement. This limited feedback constrains their pedagogical usefulness,
particularly in formative settings where revision and learning are key goals.
This highlights the need for approaches that explain model behavior and help
students understand how specific changes in writing may influence their scores.

1.4 Research Questions and Objectives 7
Within the context of AES, providing feedback that is both interpretable and
educationally meaningful presents a particular challenge. Scores alone offer
limited insight into how an essay might be improved, while feature-based or
abstract explanations often remain difficult for learners and educators to trans-
late into concrete revision actions. Counterfactual explanations offer one way
to approach this gap by focusing on minimal and targeted changes to an in-
put that would lead to a different outcome. In educational assessment, this
perspective aligns naturally with formative feedback practices, where students
are encouraged to revise their work incrementally and reflect on the effects of
specific changes. By illustrating how small modifications in writing—such as
correcting errors or refining wording—may influence predicted scores, counter-
factual explanations can serve as an intuitive bridge between model behavior
and actionable guidance.
To justify the selection of these research directions, this thesis adopts a per-
spective centered on the trustworthy use of Automated Essay Scoring in educa-
tional settings. For AES systems to be meaningfully deployed, it is not sufficient
to consider high average performance alone; rather, multiple complementary
considerations must be taken into account. First, their evaluation should rely
on metrics that meaningfully reflect reliability , as agreement scores play a gate-
keeping role in determining whether systems are considered valid and ready
for use. Second, beyond aggregate performance, AES systems should be exam-
ined for consistency at the individual level, ensuring that similar essays receive
similar scores—an expectation that is fundamental to fairness in educational as-
sessment. Third, AES systems should support interpretability and pedagogical
usefulness, enabling learners and educators to understand how scores relate to
concrete aspects of writing. In this context, counterfactual explanations repre-
sent a promising mechanism for connecting model behavior to actionable revi-
sion guidance by illustrating how specific textual changes influence predicted
scores. Together, these three dimensions—evaluation metrics, individual fair-
ness, and counterfactual feedback—form a coherent analytical framework for
examining not only whether AES systems perform well, but whether they can
be trusted, interpreted, and meaningfully used in practice.

To investigate the critical challenges surrounding the reliability , fairness, and
feedback mechanisms of AES systems, this thesis is guided by the following
research questions:

• RQ1: Does high human-computer agreement in AES indicate reliable
performance across diverse input scenarios?
While human-computer agreement is a commonly used metric to validate
AES systems, it does not necessarily ensure that the model performs well
in all circumstances. This question aims to explore whether high agree-
ment with human scorers, typically evaluated on a narrow set of inputs,
holds up across more varied and challenging essay types, such as off-topic
or gibberish responses.
• RQ2: What are the potential limitations of Quadratic Weighted Kappa
(QWK) as an evaluation metric for AES systems?
QWK is one of the most widely used metrics for evaluating AES perfor-
mance, primarily due to its ability to account for chance agreement be-
tween raters. However, this metric can be sensitive to factors such as
rating scales, class distributions, and the prevalence of certain score cat-
egories. This research question investigates these limitations, particularly
in evaluation scenarios involving multiple raters or uneven score distribu-
tions.
